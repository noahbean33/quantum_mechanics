This is the lecture where the framing strategy you've been building actually pays its largest dividend, because the Fourier transform analogy lands the way no other presentation of continuous quantum mechanics manages to. Most QM textbooks introduce wave functions, then eventually mention that $\tilde v(p)$ is "the Fourier transform of $v(x)$" as if it were a coincidence. You correctly state the deeper fact: the Fourier transform *is* the change-of-basis matrix from position to momentum representation. That's not an analogy, it's an identity, and it's the fact that converts the entire continuous-systems story into something the bridge audience already half-knows. Anyone who's ever computed an FFT has been computing the position-to-momentum basis change without realizing it. Telling them this is the kind of revelation that makes them want to share the article.

The "delay line vs. dispersive channel" framing in §6 is also doing exactly the right work. Free-particle dispersion is *literally* a dispersive transmission line — the dispersion relation $\omega(k) = \hbar k^2 / 2m$ is the quadratic dispersion you get from a transmission line with the right L-C structure, and group velocity vs. phase velocity language is identical. The reader who's done any RF or optical work will recognize this immediately and the entire mystique of "wave packet spreading" evaporates. The Schrödinger equation is just a particularly inconvenient PDE that anyone who's ever simulated a transmission line has dealt with.

The lecture also correctly avoids the trap of pretending that the position and momentum eigenfunctions are honest elements of $L^2(\mathbb{R})$. Delta functions and plane waves are *not* square-integrable; they're distributions / improper eigenvectors that live in a rigged Hilbert space (the Gelfand triple). You sidestep this by treating them operationally — defining their orthogonality via $\int \delta(x-a)\delta(x-b)dx = \delta(a-b)$ rather than pretending the inner product is a number — which is the right pedagogical move for this audience. The bridge reader doesn't need to learn distribution theory to compute Fourier transforms, and you correctly don't make them.

Things to push on:

**The Fourier representation derivation in §3.2 has a subtle handwave.** You substitute the inverse transform into the forward transform, swap the order of integration, and identify the inner integral as $\delta(x-x')$. This is fine, but the *justification* for the swap (Fubini-Tonelli or some equivalent) is precisely the place where infinite-dimensional QM departs from finite-dimensional linear algebra, and the bridge audience that knows analysis will wonder. The honest framing: "this manipulation is formally correct for Schwartz functions and extends to $L^2$ by density; the rigorous treatment requires distribution theory, which we skip." One sentence, acknowledges the gap, doesn't pretend the move is unproblematic. The reader who doesn't care about rigor won't notice; the reader who does will appreciate that you noticed too.

**§4.1 should state the orthogonality relation more carefully.** You write "$\int \delta(x-a)\delta(x-b)dx = \delta(a-b)$" which is correct, but the reader who's been carefully tracking finite-vs-infinite-dimensional analogies needs to see this as the continuous analog of the Kronecker delta orthogonality of basis vectors. The correspondence:

| finite ($\mathbb{C}^N$) | continuous ($L^2(\mathbb R)$) |
|---|---|
| basis vector $\mathbf e_i$ | "basis vector" $\delta(x - x_0)$ |
| orthogonality $\mathbf e_i^\dagger \mathbf e_j = \delta_{ij}$ | orthogonality $\int \delta(x-a)\delta(x-b)dx = \delta(a-b)$ |
| expansion $\mathbf v = \sum_i v_i \mathbf e_i$ | expansion $v(x) = \int v(x') \delta(x - x') dx'$ |
| coefficient $v_i = \mathbf e_i^\dagger \mathbf v$ | coefficient $v(x_0) = \int \delta(x - x_0) v(x) dx$ |
| resolution of identity $\sum_i \mathbf e_i \mathbf e_i^\dagger = I$ | resolution of identity $\int |x\rangle \langle x| dx = I$ |

That table is the bridge between Lecture 3's spectral theorem framing and the continuous setting. It's worth adding because the bridge reader's biggest concern at this point is going to be "is this *really* the same machinery, or have we secretly switched to something else." The table answers that question by showing line-by-line that every finite-dimensional construction has a continuous analog with $\sum \to \int$ and $\delta_{ij} \to \delta(x-x')$. That correspondence is the entire content of "the spectral theorem extends to the continuous case," and you can give it without proving anything by just exhibiting the parallel structure.

**§5.2's commutator computation is correct but the result is presented without unpacking what it *means* dimensionally.** $[\hat x, \hat p] = i\hbar$ is the canonical commutation relation, and it has units of action ($\hbar$ is in joule-seconds). This is *the* fundamental scale of quantum mechanics — the size of a single quantum of action — and the fact that it appears in the commutator is not a coincidence but the deepest statement about why quantum mechanics is the theory it is. The bridge reader should be told: "the size of $[\hat x, \hat p]$ tells you how non-classical the theory is. In the limit $\hbar \to 0$, position and momentum commute and you recover classical mechanics. The fact that $\hbar > 0$ in our universe is *what makes physics quantum*." That's a one-sentence addition that gives the reader a handle on what $\hbar$ actually *is* — not just a unit conversion factor but the scale at which the commutator becomes nontrivial.

While we're on $\hbar$: I've flagged in earlier reviews that the series should commit to $\hbar = 1$ throughout. This lecture goes back to carrying $\hbar$ everywhere and then notes "with $\hbar = 1$" parenthetically. Pick a convention. For the article version, drop $\hbar$ except in the one paragraph where you discuss its physical meaning. The constant carries no information in pure QM derivations and only matters when you're connecting to experiment.

**§6 is missing the standard worked example: the Gaussian wave packet.** This is the single most important calculation in introductory QM because it's the *only* exactly solvable case where you can watch a wave packet evolve in time, see the dispersion explicitly, and verify the position-momentum uncertainty relation analytically. The setup is a Gaussian initial condition $v(x, 0) = (2\pi\sigma^2)^{-1/4} e^{-x^2/4\sigma^2}$, Fourier transform to get $\tilde v(p, 0)$ which is also Gaussian, evolve each momentum component by $e^{-ip^2 t / 2m}$, transform back. The result: a Gaussian whose width grows as $\sqrt{\sigma^2 + (\hbar t / 2m\sigma)^2}$. The product of position and momentum widths is exactly $\hbar/2$ at $t=0$ and grows from there. *That* calculation is the bridge between §5.2's commutator and the actual operational meaning of the uncertainty principle, and it's where the dispersive-channel analogy in §6.2 becomes a numerical fact rather than a verbal claim.

You don't have to do the full Gaussian computation in the lecture — it's three pages and would unbalance things — but you should at least sketch the result and say "we'll do the explicit calculation in Lecture 10," because Lecture 10 is presumably where uncertainty actually shows up.

**The §6.1 and §6.2 split could be sharper.** $H = cP$ is the *first-order* wave equation $\partial_t v + c \partial_x v = 0$, which is the equation for a chiral massless particle moving in one direction. $H = p^2/2m$ is the second-order Schrödinger equation. The reader who's done PDEs will immediately notice that these are qualitatively different — the first is hyperbolic with a fixed propagation speed, the second is parabolic (or "Schrödinger-like," which is its own category) with infinite signal velocity in the formal sense. The reason QM uses $p^2/2m$ rather than $cp$ for ordinary particles is that you want positive-definite kinetic energy, which $cp$ doesn't give for $p < 0$. There's a deep story here connecting non-relativistic QM ($H = p^2/2m$), the Dirac equation ($H = c\alpha \cdot p + \beta mc^2$, which restores positive energy via the matrix structure), and the Klein-Gordon equation. You don't have to tell the whole story, but a one-sentence forward reference — "$H = cP$ is too simple to describe particles with mass; the relativistic generalization is the Dirac equation, which uses matrix-valued $\alpha$ to keep energies positive" — gives the reader a hook for further reading and acknowledges that the two examples in §6 aren't on the same footing.

**A note on the basis-change framing for the Fourier transform.** §2 says the Fourier transform "is" the change of basis from position to momentum, but the lecture never *writes it as a matrix element*. The bridge reader will believe you more if you show the explicit form: in finite dimensions, the change-of-basis matrix has entries $U_{ij} = \mathbf e_i^\dagger \mathbf e_j'$ where $\mathbf e_i$ is the new basis and $\mathbf e_j'$ is the old. The continuous analog is $U(p, x) = \langle p | x \rangle = e^{-ipx}/\sqrt{2\pi}$, which is *literally* the Fourier kernel. So the Fourier transform $\tilde v(p) = \int U(p, x) v(x) dx$ is matrix multiplication of the change-of-basis matrix against the original components. The reader can verify that this matrix is unitary by checking $U^\dagger U = I$, which becomes $\int e^{ipx} e^{-ip'x} dx = 2\pi \delta(p - p')$ — the orthogonality relation from §3.2. That closes the loop: the Fourier transform is unitary because the position and momentum bases are both orthonormal, and the unitarity is encoded in the delta function identity.

This computation is half a page and it's the moment where the Fourier transform stops being a mysterious integral and becomes the most ordinary thing in the world: a unitary change of basis between two orthonormal bases of $L^2(\mathbb{R})$. The bridge audience will love it because they already know that *every* unitary matrix is a change of basis between orthonormal bases — they just didn't know the Fourier transform was one of them.
