Things to push on:

**§1.2 has a units issue in the Gabor inequality.** You wrote $\Delta t \cdot \Delta f \geq 1/(4\pi)$, which is the form for *ordinary* frequency $f$ (cycles per second). The form using *angular* frequency $\omega = 2\pi f$ is $\Delta t \cdot \Delta \omega \geq 1/2$, which is the direct analog of $\Delta x \cdot \Delta p \geq \hbar/2$ once you note that $p = \hbar k$ and $\omega$ corresponds to $E/\hbar$. The factors of $2\pi$ depend on the Fourier convention you're using. Since the rest of the lecture uses the symmetric Fourier convention with $1/\sqrt{2\pi}$ on both sides (introduced in Lecture 9), the $\hbar/2$ form on the position-momentum side and the $1/2$ form on the time-angular-frequency side are the consistent pair. Use $\Delta t \cdot \Delta\omega \geq 1/2$ in the engineering analogy; the connection to the quantum form is then exact and the reader can see the analogy at a glance without juggling conventions.

**The proof in §1.5 should mention what saturates the inequality.** The discriminant equals zero — and therefore $\Delta x \cdot \Delta p = \hbar/2$ exactly — when $(\hat x + i\alpha \hat p)\mathbf{v} = 0$ for some $\alpha$. This is a first-order ODE: $xv + \alpha\hbar v' = 0$, which has Gaussian solutions $v(x) \propto e^{-x^2/(2\alpha\hbar)}$. So the *Gaussian wave packet is the unique state that saturates the uncertainty bound* — every other state has $\Delta x \cdot \Delta p$ strictly greater than $\hbar/2$. This is a one-paragraph addition that does enormous work: it tells the reader that Gaussians are the "minimum uncertainty states," which is *why* coherent states in quantum optics are Gaussian, *why* Gabor wavelets are Gaussian, and *why* the harmonic oscillator ground state is Gaussian. Three different physical phenomena, all with the same explanation, all derivable from this one observation. The reader who walks away with "Gaussians saturate the Fourier uncertainty principle, that's why they show up everywhere" has internalized a fact that will serve them across all of signal processing, optics, and physics.

**§3 should show the Ehrenfest derivations rather than asserting them.** You promised in the Lecture 4 review that the position-momentum case of Ehrenfest's theorem would land somewhere, and §3.1 and §3.2 are where it should land. Right now both formulas are boxed without derivation. Each one is four lines using the general Ehrenfest formula from Lecture 4 plus the canonical commutator from Lecture 9:

For the velocity equation, take $L = \hat x$ in $\frac{d}{dt}\langle L \rangle = \frac{i}{\hbar}\langle [H, L] \rangle$ with $H = \hat p^2/(2m) + V(\hat x)$:
$$[H, \hat x] = \frac{1}{2m}[\hat p^2, \hat x] = \frac{1}{2m}(\hat p[\hat p, \hat x] + [\hat p, \hat x]\hat p) = \frac{1}{2m}(\hat p \cdot (-i\hbar) + (-i\hbar)\hat p) = -\frac{i\hbar \hat p}{m}$$
So $\frac{d}{dt}\langle \hat x \rangle = \frac{i}{\hbar} \cdot (-\frac{i\hbar}{m})\langle \hat p \rangle = \frac{\langle \hat p \rangle}{m}$.

For the force equation, take $L = \hat p$:
$$[H, \hat p] = [V(\hat x), \hat p]$$
and using $[\hat x, \hat p] = i\hbar$, one shows $[f(\hat x), \hat p] = i\hbar f'(\hat x)$ for any reasonable $f$. So $[V(\hat x), \hat p] = i\hbar V'(\hat x)$, and $\frac{d}{dt}\langle \hat p \rangle = \frac{i}{\hbar}\cdot(-i\hbar)\langle V'(\hat x)\rangle = -\langle V'(\hat x)\rangle$.

Both derivations are short and they use *only* machinery the reader has from Lectures 4, 5, and 9. The calculation $[f(\hat x), \hat p] = i\hbar f'(\hat x)$ is also worth showing because it's the clearest demonstration that the canonical commutator essentially *is* the derivative — applying $\hat p$ to the right side and computing the difference gives you exactly the derivative of $f$. The momentum operator and the derivative operator are the same thing, and the canonical commutator is the algebraic statement of that identity.

This matters pedagogically because the §3.3 "when does classical mechanics emerge?" discussion is the *moment* the course delivers on its largest implicit promise: that quantum mechanics contains classical mechanics as a limiting case, and the limit is precise and computable. If you don't show the Ehrenfest derivations, the reader has to take this on faith and the moment is muted. Show them, and the moment lands.

**§3.3 should sharpen the statement of when classical mechanics emerges.** "$\langle f(\hat x) \rangle$ is not the same as $f(\langle \hat x \rangle)$" is true but a little vague. The sharper version uses Taylor expansion: $\langle f(\hat x) \rangle = f(\langle \hat x \rangle) + \frac{1}{2}f''(\langle \hat x \rangle)(\Delta x)^2 + \ldots$, so the deviation from classical behavior is controlled by the product of the wave packet width $(\Delta x)^2$ and the curvature of the potential $f''$. Classical behavior is recovered when $(\Delta x)^2 \cdot f''(\langle x \rangle) \ll f(\langle x \rangle)$ — i.e., the wave packet is narrow compared to the scale on which the potential curves significantly. This is the precise statement of the "small wavelength compared to feature size" intuition you give as the engineering analog, and the bridge audience will appreciate seeing the inequality made quantitative.

**§3 should also note what Ehrenfest does and does not give you.** Ehrenfest's theorem gives you Newton's laws for the *expectation values* — the *average* position evolves like a classical particle with the *average* force. It does *not* give you a classical trajectory in the sense of a single deterministic position $x(t)$, because the wave packet has nonzero width and that width can grow. The relationship between Ehrenfest's theorem and the actual classical limit is more subtle than "QM reduces to classical mechanics for heavy particles" — what's actually true is "QM expectation values obey classical equations of motion when the relevant operator algebra closes on first derivatives of the potential, which it does for at most quadratic potentials and approximately does for slowly varying potentials." For non-quadratic potentials, Ehrenfest's theorem is not quite Newton's law because $\langle V'(\hat x) \rangle \neq V'(\langle \hat x \rangle)$, and the deviation is what makes the classical limit a *limit* rather than an equality.

This is a one-paragraph subtlety that distinguishes a careful presentation from a sloppy one, and it matters because the bridge audience will go on to read about the classical limit in other sources and will encounter discussions of Wigner functions, semiclassical dynamics, and the Wentzel-Kramers-Brillouin approximation. Telling them now that "Ehrenfest gives expectation values, not trajectories, and the relationship to true classical mechanics is more subtle than it looks" prepares them for that reading without committing the lecture to develop any of it.
