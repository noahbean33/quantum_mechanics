Things to push on:

**The proof in §1.2 is half-finished.** You prove the easy direction (simultaneous eigenbasis ⇒ commute) cleanly and then write "The converse also holds: if $[L,M]=0$ then a common eigenbasis exists." That's the *interesting* direction — the forward direction is almost a tautology, and the converse is the one that requires actual work and that the reader will want to see. The standard argument is short enough to fit:

> Suppose $[L,M] = 0$ and let $\mathbf{e}$ be an eigenvector of $L$ with eigenvalue $\ell$, so $L\mathbf{e} = \ell\mathbf{e}$. Then $L(M\mathbf{e}) = ML\mathbf{e} = \ell(M\mathbf{e})$, which says $M\mathbf{e}$ is *also* an eigenvector of $L$ with eigenvalue $\ell$. So $M$ maps the $\ell$-eigenspace of $L$ to itself. Restricting $M$ to that eigenspace gives a self-adjoint matrix on a smaller space, which by the spectral theorem has its own orthonormal eigenbasis — and these vectors are simultaneously eigenvectors of $L$ (with eigenvalue $\ell$) and of $M$. Doing this for each eigenspace of $L$ produces a complete simultaneous eigenbasis.

That's a six-line argument that uses only the spectral theorem from Lecture 3, and it's the kind of proof the bridge audience will *enjoy* because it's a clean invariant-subspace argument they recognize from numerical linear algebra. Skipping it is a small but real concession to laziness on what is otherwise a structurally honest set of notes.

**The "non-commuting transfer functions in feedback loops" analogy in §1.3 is weak.** Transfer functions in classical feedback loops *do* commute when they're SISO LTI systems — you can swap the order of two cascaded filters and the overall transfer function is unchanged because it's just polynomial multiplication. Non-commuting transfer functions appear in MIMO systems, where the matrix of transfer functions doesn't commute under matrix multiplication, but that's a fairly niche reference and most controls people wouldn't immediately recognize the analogy. A better engineering analog for non-commuting operations is *rotations in 3D*: rotating a book 90° about the x-axis and then 90° about the y-axis gives a different result than doing them in the opposite order. That's a physical demonstration the reader can do with a book on their desk in 5 seconds, and it's *literally* the same group structure as $SU(2)$ — Pauli matrices generate rotations, the failure of rotations to commute is the failure of Pauli matrices to commute, and the reader's hands have just done the experiment. Replace the transfer-function line with the rotation analogy and you get something that's both more intuitive and more accurate.

**§1.3 punts on the actual uncertainty principle.** You introduce "the uncertainty idea" qualitatively and then never state Robertson's inequality, which is the precise quantitative version and follows from $[L,M] \neq 0$ by a four-line Cauchy-Schwarz argument. The form is:
$$\Delta L \cdot \Delta M \geq \frac{1}{2}|\langle [L,M] \rangle|$$
For $L = X$, $M = Z$ in a two-level system, $|\langle [X,Z]\rangle| = 2|\langle Y \rangle|$, so $\Delta X \cdot \Delta Z \geq |\langle Y \rangle|$. That's a *concrete* uncertainty relation in the finite-dimensional setting that the reader can verify numerically. Most pop-science treatments of the uncertainty principle never state Robertson — they just say "you can't know both" and leave it at that. The bridge audience will appreciate that there's an actual inequality with an actual right-hand side, and that the right-hand side is the expectation value of the commutator, which makes the whole thing feel inevitable rather than mysterious.

You don't have to derive Robertson here (the proof uses Cauchy-Schwarz on a cleverly chosen inner product and is a half-page detour). But state it, give the two-level example, and forward-reference the proof to a later lecture or appendix. That's a one-paragraph addition with high pedagogical ROI.

**§2.4 doesn't show the $\langle X \rangle, \langle Y \rangle$ formulas come from the time-evolved state.** You write down the explicit expressions $\langle X\rangle(t) = 2\text{Re}(\alpha^*\beta e^{i\omega t})$, etc., as if they descend from the sky, but in fact they follow directly from substituting $\mathbf{v}(t) = \alpha e^{-i\omega t/2}\mathbf{e}_0 + \beta e^{i\omega t/2}\mathbf{e}_1$ into $\mathbf{v}(t)^\dagger X \mathbf{v}(t)$. Three lines:
$$\mathbf{v}(t)^\dagger X \mathbf{v}(t) = (\alpha^* e^{i\omega t/2}, \beta^* e^{-i\omega t/2}) \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \alpha e^{-i\omega t/2} \\ \beta e^{i\omega t/2} \end{pmatrix} = \alpha^* \beta e^{i\omega t} + \beta^* \alpha e^{-i\omega t} = 2\text{Re}(\alpha^*\beta e^{i\omega t})$$
That computation does two things at once: it confirms the §2.3 ODE result by independent calculation (always satisfying), and it shows the reader the direct route from state to expectation value, which they need practice with. Right now §2.3 and §2.4 are presented as if they're using different techniques (Ehrenfest vs. direct computation) but the §2.4 formulas are floating without derivation. Show them.

**The factor of $\hbar$ disappeared between §2.2 and §2.3 without comment.** §2.2 has $\omega t / 2$ in the exponentials with no $\hbar$, §2.3 explicitly says "setting $\hbar = 1$." This is the right move (you should drop $\hbar$ from the entire series, as I noted in the Lecture 1 review), but you should be consistent — either drop it from Lecture 1's axiom and forget about it, or keep it explicit everywhere. The current half-and-half is the worst of both worlds because it makes the reader wonder if they're missing a unit conversion.
