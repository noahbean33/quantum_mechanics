**§3.2 has the most important sentence in the lecture and it's hidden.** "The magnitudes $|\alpha_k|$ do not change — all time dependence is in the relative phases." This is the entire conceptual content of unitary evolution and it deserves its own paragraph, not a parenthetical. The reader needs to understand: in the energy eigenbasis, time evolution is *trivial* — it's just N independent phase rotations at N different frequencies. Every single quantum dynamical phenomenon (Rabi oscillations, interference, beats, revivals, the entire structure of quantum dynamics) is a consequence of those phase rotations being at *different rates* and the observable being expressed in a *different basis* than the energy eigenbasis. That's the whole game.

The sentence to add: "If you measure an observable that commutes with H, you'll see no dynamics — the eigenbasis of H is also the eigenbasis of your observable, and the relative phases cancel in $|e_i^\dagger v|^2$. All quantum dynamics comes from measuring observables whose eigenbasis is *misaligned* with the energy eigenbasis. Misalignment plus phase rotation equals interference equals dynamics." That's a paragraph that converts the formula into a worldview, and it's the thing the bridge audience will remember six months later.

**§3 needs a worked example and it's the obvious one: Rabi oscillations on a qubit.** You have all the machinery — set $H = \frac{\omega}{2} X$, take initial state $\mathbf{e}_0$, expand in the eigenbasis of X (which you derived in Lecture 2), apply the phase rotations, transform back, compute $\langle Z \rangle(t)$. Result: $\langle Z \rangle(t) = \cos(\omega t)$. That's the simplest possible quantum oscillation, it uses *exclusively* machinery the reader already has, and it produces a recognizable cosine that they can plot. Without a worked example, §3 is just abstract formulas. With this one example, §3 is the moment the reader sees a quantum system actually *do* something for the first time.

The Colab cell writes itself:
```python
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy.linalg import expm

X = np.array([[0,1],[1,0]])
Z = np.array([[1,0],[0,-1]])
H = 0.5 * X
v0 = np.array([1, 0])

ts = np.linspace(0, 4*np.pi, 200)
zs = [np.real(np.conj(expm(-1j*H*t) @ v0) @ Z @ (expm(-1j*H*t) @ v0)) for t in ts]

plt.plot(ts, zs)
```
Twelve lines, produces a cosine, and the reader has now *simulated their first quantum system*. That moment is the entire pedagogical payoff of the four lectures so far. Don't leave it on the table.

**§4 buries the lede on inner product preservation.** "States that start orthogonal stay orthogonal" is correct but understated. The deeper fact is: *quantum evolution cannot create or destroy distinguishability*. Two states that you couldn't tell apart at $t=0$ remain indistinguishable forever; two states you could perfectly distinguish remain perfectly distinguishable forever. This is the precise sense in which unitary evolution is "information-preserving," and it's the foundation for *all* of quantum information theory — no-cloning, the no-deleting theorem, the entire reason quantum error correction is hard, the reason Hawking's information paradox is a paradox in the first place. You don't have to develop any of that here, but one sentence framing the result as "distinguishability is conserved" rather than "overlaps are preserved" gives the reader a much sharper handle.

**The Ehrenfest equation in §5.2 should explicitly connect to classical mechanics.** You note in §5.2 that the result is "the bridge between quantum evolution and classical equations of motion" but you don't show the bridge. The standard demonstration: take $H = \frac{p^2}{2m} + V(x)$ in a basis where position and momentum operators exist, and derive that $\frac{d}{dt}\langle x \rangle = \frac{\langle p \rangle}{m}$ and $\frac{d}{dt}\langle p \rangle = -\langle V'(x) \rangle$. These are *literally Hamilton's equations for the expectation values*. That's the moment where a physics student first sees that quantum mechanics contains classical mechanics as a limit, and it's a transformative result.

The complication: you don't have position and momentum operators yet because you're still in the finite-dimensional setting. Two options. (a) Punt on this until the lecture where you introduce continuous systems and forward-reference: "In a future lecture we'll see that for a particle with $H = p^2/2m + V(x)$, the Ehrenfest equations reduce to Hamilton's equations for $\langle x \rangle$ and $\langle p \rangle$ — quantum mechanics literally contains classical mechanics as the equation of motion for expectation values." (b) Do a finite-dimensional analog with a two-level system: take $H = \frac{\Omega}{2} Z$ (a constant magnetic field along z), compute $\frac{d}{dt}\langle X \rangle$ and $\frac{d}{dt}\langle Y \rangle$, and show that they obey the precession equations $\dot X = -\Omega Y$, $\dot Y = \Omega X$, which is *exactly* the Bloch precession that NMR engineers know. That's a finite-dimensional analog of "quantum reproduces classical" and it lands hard with the engineering-flavored audience because Bloch precession shows up in MRI, NMR spectroscopy, and atomic clocks — places they've heard of.

I'd push for option (b). It uses only machinery you have, it produces a result the audience may have encountered in another context, and it concretizes the otherwise-abstract Ehrenfest formula.

**The unitarity-from-Hermiticity proof in §1.2 is doing double duty with §2 and the order is slightly muddled.** §1.2 proves "if H is Hermitian then U is unitary." §2 uses unitarity to derive the ODE. But the *promised* derivation from Lecture 1 was the other direction: "norm preservation ⇒ unitarity ⇒ Hermitian generator." Your proof in §1.2 establishes "Hermitian ⇒ unitary," which is the converse. The infinitesimal calculation $I + \frac{i\epsilon}{\hbar}(H^\dagger - H) = I \Rightarrow H^\dagger = H$ actually does go in the right direction (from $U^\dagger U = I$ to $H^\dagger = H$), but you've labeled it as proving "if H Hermitian then U unitary," which is backward.

Fix the framing: state the theorem as "$U(t) = e^{-iHt/\hbar}$ is unitary if and only if $H$ is self-adjoint" (which is what you actually prove) and then explicitly note that the *physics* direction is unitarity-implies-Hermitian: we *demand* unitarity (because probability must be conserved), and Hermitian H is the consequence, not the assumption. The reader who's paying attention will catch this and lose a little trust if you don't get the logical direction right.
