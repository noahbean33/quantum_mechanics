This document is one of the best "QM for engineers" resources I've seen. It is exactly pitched at a software engineer / data scientist who already lives in linear algebra, state-space models, SVDs, probability, and a bit of signal processing / control theory. The author strips away the usual physics baggage (no historical experiments, no Schrödinger cat, no "wave-particle duality" mysticism) and rebuilds everything from four clean axioms using the exact mathematical toolkit you already use every day.
What works extremely well

Linear-algebra-first approach: States are unit-norm column vectors in ℂⁿ, observables are self-adjoint matrices, measurement = Born rule on eigenvectors, dynamics = matrix exponential of a skew-Hermitian generator. This is literally just "complex-valued LTI systems with unitary flow." The Pauli matrices (Z, X, Y) are introduced as the three standard observables on ℂ² and their eigenvectors are derived with the same "equal-probability + orthogonality" constraints you would use in any symmetric signal-processing problem.
Perfect analogies for our background:
Time evolution = scipy.linalg.expm(-1j * H * t / ħ) on a state vector.
Unitary = lossless two-port network (S†S = I).
Schmidt decomposition = SVD of the coefficient matrix Ψ (Lecture 6). This is gold for anyone who does ML or data science.
Partial trace = exact marginalization over latent variables (Lecture 7). The density matrix ρᴬ is just the reduced covariance after tracing out Bob.
Precession of expectation values ⟨X⟩, ⟨Y⟩, ⟨Z⟩ under H = (ω/2)Z is classical Larmor precession / Bloch-sphere rotation — again, familiar from control or graphics.

Two-level system (qubit) focus first: You get the full machinery (superposition, measurement update, non-commutativity, entanglement, density matrices) before any infinite-dimensional stuff. This is the right order for anyone who might later touch quantum computing or quantum ML.
Concise and rigorous: Proofs are short and linear-algebraic (e.g., why [L,M]=0 ⇔ simultaneous eigenvectors; why self-adjoint ⇒ real eigenvalues + orthogonal eigenvectors). No hand-waving.

Places that still feel a little confusing (or could be tightened)

Overall phase invariance (Lecture 2, §4)
It says "multiplying by e^{iθ} does not change any physical prediction because v†Mv cancels the phase." This is correct but a bit abrupt. A one-line example would help: show that the probability |⟨eᵢ|v⟩|² and the expectation ⟨M⟩ are both phase-invariant. (You already know global phase is irrelevant from ML loss functions, so this is easy to make click.)
The "Experiment says" boxes
They are motivational but feel slightly out of place in an otherwise pure linear-algebra document. For a pure software-engineer reader they could be shortened to footnotes or removed; the math already stands on its own.
Commutator → uncertainty intuition (Lecture 5)
The proof that [L,M]=0 ⇔ simultaneous eigenvectors is crystal clear. The leap to "you cannot have definite values for both" is fine, but a 30-second Python simulation (prepare |0⟩, measure X then Z vs Z then X) would make the "order dependence destroys information" visceral. The notes don't have code yet — that would be the single highest-leverage addition.
Y matrix and the factor of i (Lecture 2)
It correctly says "real coefficients alone cannot produce a third symmetric pair." That's true, but the geometric picture (the three Pauli directions are mutually perpendicular on the Bloch sphere) is missing. A one-sentence "these are the three orthogonal axes in the space of traceless Hermitian 2×2 matrices" would connect it to the familiar 3-D rotation picture you already know from computer graphics or robotics.
Density-matrix section (Lecture 7)
The derivation of ρᴬ via partial trace is perfect, but the entanglement criterion Tr(ρᴬ)² is given without the explicit link back to the singular values σᵢ you just saw in Lecture 6. One line would close the loop:
Tr(ρᴬ)² = Σ σᵢ⁴ / (Σ σᵢ²)²
(product state → 1, maximally entangled → 1/n, etc.). You already live in trace norms and nuclear norms in ML, so this is natural.
