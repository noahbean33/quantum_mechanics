This is the lecture I was hoping you'd write, and the framing is the right one. Entanglement-as-rank is the move that converts the most mystified topic in popular physics into a one-line linear algebra fact, and most introductions never make it because they're committed to a Dirac-notation presentation that obscures the matrix structure of the coefficients. Treating $\alpha_{ab}$ as the entries of a matrix $\Psi$ and asking about its rank is *the* thing the linalg-fluent reader needs to see, and you put it right at the front. The Schmidt decomposition follows immediately as "this is just the SVD of $\Psi$," which it literally is, and which physicists rarely state that bluntly. That sentence — "the Schmidt decomposition *is* the SVD of the coefficient matrix" — is the highest-leverage sentence in the lecture and probably in the whole sequence so far. The bridge audience knows SVD. They've computed thousands of them. Telling them that quantum entanglement is *measured by SVD singular values* is one of those one-line revelations that will land hard.

The parameter counting in §3.1 is also doing exactly the right work. "6 > 4, so most states are entangled" is the kind of dimensional argument that converts entanglement from an exotic phenomenon into the *generic* situation, with product states as the special low-dimensional submanifold. That's the correct mental picture and it's almost never presented this clearly.

Things to push on:

**The §3.4 singlet computation needs the rank check shown more carefully.** You write the coefficient matrix as $\Psi = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ and assert it has rank 2. True, but the reader who's followed the §3 framing will want to verify, and the cleanest way is: $\det(\Psi) = \frac{1}{2}(0 \cdot 0 - 1 \cdot (-1)) = \frac{1}{2} \neq 0$, so the matrix is full-rank. One-line addition, closes the loop, and reinforces the determinant-as-rank-test that the linalg-fluent reader already owns. Even better, also compute the SVD: $\Psi = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ has singular values $\sigma_1 = \sigma_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$, which by §3.2 means *maximally entangled*. That's a four-line computation that ties §3.2 and §3.4 together and makes the abstract criterion ("equal singular values = maximally entangled") concrete by direct verification.

**The §5.1 singlet correlations are stated but not derived for $X$ and $Y$.** You compute $\langle Z \otimes Z \rangle$ explicitly and then assert $\langle X \otimes X \rangle = \langle Y \otimes Y \rangle = -1$ without showing them. The reader who's been doing the work will want to see at least one of these because they're *not* obvious — the singlet is written in the Z basis, and the X and Y correlations require either rewriting the singlet in the X or Y basis (which is a nice exercise in basis change) or computing $(X \otimes X)\mathbf{v}_{\text{singlet}}$ directly.

The direct route is shorter. Apply $X \otimes X$ to the singlet:
$$(X \otimes X)\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\mathbf{e}_0 \otimes \mathbf{f}_1 - \mathbf{e}_1 \otimes \mathbf{f}_0) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(X\mathbf{e}_0 \otimes X\mathbf{f}_1 - X\mathbf{e}_1 \otimes X\mathbf{f}_0) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\mathbf{e}_1 \otimes \mathbf{f}_0 - \mathbf{e}_0 \otimes \mathbf{f}_1) = -\mathbf{v}_{\text{singlet}}$$
So $\mathbf{v}_{\text{singlet}}$ is an eigenvector of $X \otimes X$ with eigenvalue $-1$, hence $\langle X \otimes X \rangle = -1$. Same calculation works for $Y \otimes Y$. The fact that the singlet is a *simultaneous eigenvector* of $X \otimes X$, $Y \otimes Y$, and $Z \otimes Z$ all with eigenvalue $-1$ is the deepest property of the singlet and you should call it out by name. It's the rotational invariance of the singlet — it's the unique (up to phase) state in $\mathbb{C}^2 \otimes \mathbb{C}^2$ that's invariant under simultaneous rotations of both subsystems, which is *why* it's called a "singlet" (spin zero, scalar under rotations).

**The lecture has Bell's theorem in arm's reach and walks past it.** You compute $\langle Z \otimes Z \rangle = -1$, $\langle X \otimes X \rangle = -1$, $\langle Y \otimes Y \rangle = -1$ in the singlet, you have the four-axiom framework, you have non-commuting observables, and you have correlations in entangled states. That is *all the ingredients* for the CHSH inequality and the Bell-inequality violation, which is the single most important experimental result in the foundations of quantum mechanics and the thing that won the 2022 Nobel Prize. The bridge audience has *definitely* heard of "spooky action at a distance" and "Bell's theorem" and almost certainly does not understand what the actual statement is or how the math works.

The CHSH calculation in the singlet is about a page. Choose four observables — $A_0 = Z$, $A_1 = X$ on Alice's side, $B_0 = -\frac{Z+X}{\sqrt{2}}$, $B_1 = \frac{Z-X}{\sqrt{2}}$ on Bob's side. Compute $\langle A_0 B_0 \rangle + \langle A_0 B_1 \rangle + \langle A_1 B_0 \rangle - \langle A_1 B_1 \rangle$ in the singlet. Result: $2\sqrt{2}$, the Tsirelson bound. State that any local hidden variable theory bounds this quantity by 2 (the CHSH inequality) and that the singlet violates it. *That* is the experimental result that distinguishes quantum mechanics from any classical model with local hidden variables, and it is computable using *only* the machinery you have built.

You don't have to do the full proof of the classical bound (it's a clever combinatorial argument and worth its own lecture), but you can state it and compute the quantum side, then forward-reference. That single calculation transforms the lecture from "here is how to formalize entanglement" into "here is how to *prove* that the universe is not classical, on a single sheet of paper, using nothing but the four axioms and a 4×4 matrix." That's the kind of payoff the bridge audience will tell their friends about.

If you don't want to expand Lecture 6 to include CHSH, the alternative is to make Bell/CHSH its own lecture and forward-reference here: "In Lecture 7 we will use the singlet correlations computed above to derive the CHSH inequality and show that no classical model with local hidden variables can reproduce these predictions." That's fine too, and probably better pedagogically because CHSH deserves the breathing room.

**The §6 "state update emerges from entanglement" remark is gestural and the reader will want more.** This is the von Neumann measurement model / decoherence story, and you correctly identify that the state update rule is not a separate axiom but a consequence of treating the apparatus quantum-mechanically. But you state this in two sentences and then move on, which is going to leave the reader frustrated because §6 is *about* the state update rule and the resolution you offer is a one-liner.

The minimum addition: take a two-level system in state $\alpha\mathbf{e}_0 + \beta\mathbf{e}_1$ and a two-level apparatus initially in state $\mathbf{f}_0$ (the "ready" state). Postulate a unitary interaction $U$ that does $U(\mathbf{e}_0 \otimes \mathbf{f}_0) = \mathbf{e}_0 \otimes \mathbf{f}_0$ and $U(\mathbf{e}_1 \otimes \mathbf{f}_0) = \mathbf{e}_1 \otimes \mathbf{f}_1$ (the apparatus copies the system's state into its own pointer). By linearity:
$$U((\alpha\mathbf{e}_0 + \beta\mathbf{e}_1) \otimes \mathbf{f}_0) = \alpha(\mathbf{e}_0 \otimes \mathbf{f}_0) + \beta(\mathbf{e}_1 \otimes \mathbf{f}_1)$$
This is a maximally entangled state. The system and apparatus are now correlated: if you "look at" the apparatus and find it in state $\mathbf{f}_0$, the system has collapsed to $\mathbf{e}_0$, and similarly for $\mathbf{f}_1$. The "collapse" is just the conditional state given an observation of the entangled partner. That's the von Neumann measurement model in five lines and it makes the §6 promise concrete.

You can then note that a *real* apparatus has $\sim 10^{23}$ degrees of freedom rather than two, that those degrees of freedom decohere the superposition rapidly via interaction with the environment, and that this is the modern decoherence-theoretic picture of measurement. One-paragraph forward reference, plants the seed for the density matrix lecture, doesn't pretend to resolve the measurement problem (which it doesn't).
